The Q'eswachaka Suspension Bridge

Located on the Apurimac River and made of grasses, vines and other perishable materials, the Q'eswachaka is the last remaining Inka rope bridge that continues to be used to this day.

Because of the materials, the bridge has to be regularly maintained and local communites gather to do so every summer together as a way to honor Pachamama (Mother Earth) and their ansestors turning it into a day of ritual, dance and feasting.

Corn: An Andean Staple

Corn or Maize, a food native to Abya Yala has gone on to revolutionize cuisines around the world, now being used in other cuisines worldwide like Italian, Ghanian, Romanian and more. Anyone who has grown up with family in the Andes knows about the importance of corn with food like arepas (corn patties), empanadas (With a maize shell), mazamorra (a Corn soup) and Tamales being staples.

A Bowl of Mazamorra sered in the Colombian Andes

While these days the most familiar color of corn is yellow corn, there are many other Colors like red, black and most revered by the Inkans, white.

Atahualpa: The Last Inka

Atahualpa was the last ruler of what was known as the Inkan Empire (with Tupac Amaru being the ruler only of the Neo-Inkan state.) Ruling from Quito in what's now Ecuador, he spent most of his rule fighting with his half brother, Huascar in the Inka civil war before the Spaniards arrived.

Trying to get them on his side to defeat his brother, the Spaniards would instead capture and execute Atahualpa a year after his acension and burying his body parts across the kingdom.

The beheading of Atahualpa

Atahualpa lives on in the the legend of the Inkarri, based on his words that he would be back to avenge his death by reclaiming his kingdom and restoring harmony to Pachamama.
